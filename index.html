Girlboss or Tradwife?

Definitions, celebrity examples, and an interactive sorting tool for girlboss discourse

This tool maps both the girlboss and the tradwife on a shared spectrum, because each archetype is most legible in relation to the other.

The purpose is to make assumptions explicit: which circumstances, values, and household arrangements make someone look more like one archetype, the other, both, or neither?

Definitions
Girlboss

A girlboss is a woman who participates in the paid labor force and prioritizes paid work, career achievement, and leadership over family formation and domestic responsibilities, defining success through professional advancement and typically positioning herself as an equal or primary economic contributor within a household.

Tradwife

A tradwife is a married woman who prioritizes family formation and household management over career advancement, typically organizing her work and economic activity—often as a secondary earner—around supporting a traditional division of labor in which she takes primary responsibility for domestic responsibilities.

Contradiction zone

A woman who is both economically engaged and domestically oriented—employed and earning, but married with children and holding traditional domestic values—scores high on both axes. Neither archetype alone captures her profile.

Ambiguous zone

A woman who fits neither archetype clearly—because she is not economically central but also not domestically oriented, or because she is in a transitional life stage—sits in the ambiguous zone.

About the categories

Each profile is evaluated across seven categories — five measuring life circumstances and two measuring beliefs and values. Each option carries a tradwife score and a girlboss score from 1 (no signal) to 5 (strong signal). Your scores are averaged across all selected categories.

Life circumstances

Work and economic role
The most direct behavioral signal in the framework. Combines employment status with economic contribution, because the two together are more informative than either alone.
Individual income
Measures the woman’s own earned income, not household income. High individual income is a girlboss signal because it reflects her own economic independence. No individual income is a tradwife signal because it indicates economic dependence regardless of household wealth.
Marital status
Marriage is a strong circumstantial tradwife signal. The tradwife archetype is organized around marriage as institution and domestic partnership. The divorced or separated option carries a distinct profile — family formation that did not persist — not well captured by other variables.
Children
Number of children is among the most reliable single circumstantial signals. Three or more children is a particularly strong tradwife signal. No children is scored as a girlboss lean regardless of stated preference — intention does not determine which archetype will materialize.
Education
Correlates with career orientation but is a potential signal rather than a behavioral one. A bachelor’s degree is a mild girlboss lean (the modal credential). A graduate degree is a stronger lean as a costly career-commitment signal.

Beliefs and values

Self-identification and goals
The most direct measure of archetype designation in the framework. How a woman narrates her own life and what she is working toward is directly indicative in a way no circumstantial variable is. The middle option explicitly names the contradiction rather than forcing anyone who genuinely inhabits both into a misrepresentative response.
Beliefs about domestic labor
Who should manage the home, and under what conditions, is one of the most defensible single-question proxies for this distinction. It captures normative orientation independently of circumstances — a woman can be fully employed and hold the most traditional domestic labor beliefs, and a stay-at-home mother can believe in equal contribution in principle.
Example profiles

Girlboss — three clear examples

Girlboss
The young professional
Single, no children and no desire for them, employed full-time as a primary earner, individual income over $75k, graduate degree. Self-identification: career and professional achievement are central. Domestic labor belief: should be outsourced so both partners can do paid work. Girlboss on every variable without exception.
Girlboss
The established professional
Divorced or separated, one or two children, employed full-time as a primary earner, individual income over $75k, bachelor’s degree. Self-identification: career is central, though family is present. Domestic labor belief: both partners should contribute equally regardless of paid work. Girlboss primarily through economic independence and self-identification.
Girlboss
The cohabiting professional
Cohabiting, no children, employed full-time as a co-primary earner, individual income $35k–$75k, graduate degree. Self-identification: career, family, and household are all central. Domestic labor belief: both partners should contribute equally if paid work is roughly equal. Girlboss on all circumstantial variables.

Tradwife — three clear examples

Tradwife
The stay-at-home married mother
Married, three or more children, not working and not a household earner, no individual income, high school diploma or less. Self-identification: domestic life and family management are her primary role and goal. Domestic labor belief: should be primarily one partner’s responsibility when the other’s primary role is earning. Tradwife on every variable without exception.
Tradwife
The educated opt-out mother
Married, one or two children, not working and not a household earner, no individual income, bachelor’s degree. Self-identification: family and household come first. Domestic labor belief: should be primarily one partner’s responsibility when the other’s primary role is earning. Tradwife on every variable despite a bachelor’s degree. This is the opt-out profile — an educated woman who has left professional life for domestic life by choice.
Tradwife
The young married mother
Married, one or two children, not currently working but wants to return, some college. Self-identification: work supplements marriage, family, and household as her defining identities and goals. Domestic labor belief: both partners should contribute equally if paid work is roughly equal. Tradwife by clear preponderance — family primacy, domestic circumstances, married with children — but a softer tradwife. A useful illustration that the archetype includes a range, not just its most extreme expression.

Ambiguous — one example

Neither
The transitional case
Single, no children, employed part-time as a secondary earner, individual income under $35k, some college. Self-identification: work is part of my life but not my defining identity or primary goal. Domestic labor belief: both partners should contribute equally if paid work is roughly equal. Genuinely ambiguous. Not economically central, but attached to the labor force. Single, which leans girlboss circumstantially, but self-identification explicitly disavows career primacy. Likely in a transitional stage from which she could move in either direction.

Contradiction — one example

Both
The professional mother with traditional beliefs
Married, one or two children, employed full-time as a co-primary earner, individual income over $75k, bachelor’s degree. Self-identification: work supplements marriage, family, and household as her defining identities and goals. Domestic labor belief: should be primarily one partner’s responsibility when the other’s primary role is earning. Both scores land above the midpoint simultaneously. She earns well but has organized her working life around her family rather than organizing her family around her work. Neither archetype captures her fully.

All categories are weighted equally. The final tradwife score and girlboss score are simple averages: add all tradwife scores for selected categories and divide by the number of categories selected; repeat for girlboss. The quadrant boundary is 3.0 on each axis — a score of 3.0 or above places a profile in that archetype’s half of the axis. A profile can score above 3.0 on both axes simultaneously, placing it in the contradiction zone.

This interactive, built by Carolyn D. Gorman using Claude Sonnet 4.6 and ChatGPT, is a conceptual and illustrative exercise. It is not, nor is it intended to be, a serious academic, scholarly, or statistical classification tool. The placements of public figures are based on entirely subjective approximations based on public-facing information and branding, and the women shown did not participate in, review, or endorse the tool. This tool aims to recognize that women are not the caricature labels of either girlboss and tradwife and that no woman's value should be lowered by their potential need and choice to live out a modified version of their preferences in order to balance priorities and changing circumstances.
